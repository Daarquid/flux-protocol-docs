1.1 — Introduction

What is FLUX?

The first fused stablecoin liquidity market — where trading, lending, and FX hedging share a single capital pool.

FLUX is a decentralised finance protocol built natively on Circle's Arc blockchain. It combines a stablecoin exchange, a money market, and an institutional foreign exchange hedging system into one unified liquidity pool — removing the need for separate protocols, fragmented capital, and expensive intermediaries.

At its core, FLUX is built around a single architectural idea: that the same pool of capital can simultaneously serve traders exchanging stablecoins, liquidity providers earning yield, borrowers seeking leverage, and corporate treasury teams protecting themselves from currency risk. These participants do not compete. They make each other more profitable by sharing the same infrastructure.

The protocol is powered by the Fused Trade Ledger (FTL) — a novel AMM design that segments liquidity across discrete price bins and makes idle capital available for lending automatically. This architecture, combined with Circle's StableFX engine for institutional FX execution, creates a protocol capable of serving DeFi-native users and traditional finance institutions simultaneously.

Plain English

Think of FLUX as a shared pool of money that does three jobs at once. It lets people swap currencies. It automatically lends idle capital to earn interest. And it lets businesses protect themselves from currency risk — automatically, cheaply, and without a bank. Everyone who puts money in earns from all three activities at the same time.

The Three Pillars of FLUX

FLUX is not three products stitched together. It is one protocol with three emergent capabilities that arise from the same pool architecture.

Pillar 01

Fused Liquidity

A single pool per currency pair handles swapping, lending, and hedging simultaneously. LP capital works across all three functions without being split across separate contracts or protocols.

Pillar 02

Bin-Based Precision

Liquidity is distributed across discrete exchange rate bins. Every interaction — a swap, a borrow, a limit order, a stop-loss — targets a specific rate level. Capital at each bin earns fees from every interaction that touches it.

Pillar 03

Institutional FX Access

Corporate treasuries and fintechs connect to FLUX through programmable hedging vaults. They set their FX exposure rules once and the protocol executes automatically through Arc's StableFX engine — 24/7, sub-second, no bank required.

The Result

Mutual Compounding

LPs earn more because institutional volume flows through their capital. Institutions get better execution because LP depth is greater. Traders get tighter spreads because both sides grow the pool. Every participant amplifies the value of every other.

Who Builds With FLUX?

FLUX is designed to serve three distinct user types whose needs are different but whose capital is mutually dependent.

Liquidity Providers

DeFi-native participants who deposit stablecoins into the pool and earn yield. Unlike traditional AMMs where capital earns only from swap fees, FLUX LPs earn simultaneously from trading activity, lending utilisation, and institutional hedge execution fees. Idle capital — the portion not currently being traded against — is automatically deployed into lending, meaning there is no such thing as inactive capital in FLUX.

DeFi Traders

Users who interact with the pool to exchange currencies, speculate on exchange rate movements, or execute automated orders. The bin architecture enables limit orders (capital that executes when the market hits a target rate), stop-loss positions (automatic protection against adverse rate moves), long and short positions on currency pairs, and looped leverage strategies — all using the same borrowing mechanics that also power institutional hedging.

Corporate Treasuries & Institutions

Businesses and financial institutions holding stablecoins across multiple currency jurisdictions. A company that earns in euros but pays in dollars faces FX risk every day. FLUX lets them configure a hedging vault with their specific parameters — exposure limits, rate thresholds, rebalancing triggers — and the vault monitors and executes automatically. What currently costs 2–5% through a bank and takes 3–5 days to settle becomes a programmable, sub-second, always-on treasury function.

1.2 — Introduction

Why Arc?

FLUX is built natively on Circle's Arc blockchain — not as a deployment choice made for convenience, but because Arc is the only chain with the exact combination of infrastructure that makes FLUX possible. Each core feature of FLUX depends on a specific Arc capability. Without Arc, the protocol as designed cannot exist.

This section explains what Arc provides, why each element is non-negotiable for FLUX, and why building on any other chain would require fundamental architectural compromises.

Sub-Second Finality

Arc uses the Malachite consensus engine — a Byzantine Fault Tolerant consensus mechanism developed by the team from Informal Systems — to achieve deterministic transaction finality in under one second. Benchmarks show approximately 780 milliseconds for 100 validators with 1MB blocks.

For FLUX, this is not a performance preference. It is a hard requirement. FX exchange rates move continuously. When a corporate treasury vault is configured to hedge exposure if EURC drops below 1.05, the execution must happen at 1.05 — not 1.049 three seconds later after the market has already moved. When a trader places a limit order at a specific bin, that order must execute the moment the market touches that bin. Probabilistic finality on chains like Ethereum — where transactions can theoretically be reversed until multiple blocks have passed — introduces settlement risk that makes precise bin-level execution unreliable. Arc's deterministic finality eliminates this entirely.

Technical Note

Arc's finality is deterministic, not probabilistic. This means once a transaction is confirmed on Arc, it is final and irreversible immediately. There is no waiting for block confirmations. For StableFX settlement, this means both legs of a currency swap either settle atomically in the same instant or neither settles — eliminating counterparty risk entirely.

USDC as Native Gas

On Arc, all transaction fees are denominated in and paid in USDC. There is no native volatile gas token. This design choice has profound implications for FLUX's institutional use case.

A corporate treasury operating automated hedging vaults executes potentially thousands of transactions per day — rebalances, vault adjustments, hedge executions, collateral management. On Ethereum or Solana, gas fees fluctuate with network congestion. During volatile market periods — exactly when treasury teams most need to execute hedges — gas costs spike. A CFO cannot budget for a treasury function with unpredictable, volatile operating costs.

Arc's USDC-denominated gas means the cost of every vault operation is predictable, denominated in the same currency as the treasury positions themselves, and stable regardless of network activity. This transforms FLUX's vault system from a crypto-native curiosity into a credible institutional treasury tool.

Circle StableFX Engine

Circle StableFX is an institutional-grade FX engine built natively into Arc. It enables 24/7 stablecoin-to-stablecoin currency conversion with onchain settlement, using an off-chain Request for Quote (RFQ) process for price discovery and on-chain Payment-vs-Payment (PvP) settlement for atomic execution.

FLUX's hedging vault system sits directly on top of StableFX. When a vault needs to execute a hedge — converting EURC exposure to USDC at a target rate — it routes through StableFX for execution and settles on Arc. FLUX does not need to build an FX engine from scratch. It builds programmable strategy logic on top of institutional-grade FX infrastructure that Circle already operates and maintains. This is a fundamental architectural advantage.

Access Requirement

StableFX is a permissioned product. Institutional access to StableFX requires completing Circle's KYB and AML verification process. DeFi-native pool interactions (swaps, borrowing, LP provision) do not require this. Only institutional vault features that route through StableFX carry this requirement.

Opt-In Privacy

Arc includes a privacy subsystem exposed through an EVM precompile that routes to pluggable cryptographic backends. This allows transaction details — positions, balances, counterparty data — to be shielded from public view while remaining fully auditable to authorised parties such as regulators and auditors.

For corporate treasury teams, this is a non-negotiable compliance requirement. An enterprise treasury team cannot expose their full currency positions, hedge sizes, and rebalancing activity to a public blockchain. Arc's opt-in privacy allows institutional vault operators to maintain operational confidentiality while meeting their regulatory disclosure obligations through selective auditability.

EVM Compatibility

Arc is fully compatible with the Ethereum Virtual Machine. Developers build on Arc using Solidity, Foundry, and Hardhat — the same tools used on Ethereum. Arc targets the Prague EVM hard fork, meaning most existing Ethereum smart contracts port to Arc with minimal modifications, primarily accounting for USDC denomination instead of ETH.

For FLUX's development, this means the concentrated liquidity AMM patterns from Uniswap v3 and LiquidBook, the debt NFT patterns from existing DeFi money markets, and the vault logic patterns from yield protocol architecture are all directly portable. FLUX builds on a decade of Ethereum DeFi engineering knowledge without inheriting Ethereum's performance limitations.

Institutional Distribution

Arc launched its public testnet in October 2025 with over 100 institutional participants including Visa, BlackRock, Goldman Sachs, Deutsche Bank, Standard Chartered, BNY, State Street, Coinbase, Kraken, and Robinhood. In May 2026, Circle closed a $222 million Arc token presale led by a16z, with BlackRock, Apollo, ICE, ARK Invest, and others participating at a $3 billion fully diluted valuation.

These are not passive endorsements. These are the exact organisations — global banks, asset managers, payment networks, and exchanges — whose treasury teams are FLUX's primary institutional customers. FLUX is built inside a network already engaged in selling to its own target market. The cold-start distribution problem that kills most DeFi protocols is structurally de-risked by building natively on Arc.

1.3 — Introduction

The Problem We Solve

FLUX addresses five distinct but structurally related problems that exist across DeFi and institutional finance. These are not adjacent problems bolted together under one roof. They share a common root cause — capital that is forced to do one job at a time — and a common solution: a fused pool that makes the same capital serve multiple functions simultaneously.

Understanding these problems clearly is important because FLUX's design decisions — bins, auto-lending, the FTL, the vault system — are all direct responses to them. The architecture is not arbitrary. Every piece exists to solve something specific.

1

Liquidity Fragmentation

In the current DeFi landscape, a project that wants to provide trading, lending, and yield on the same asset pair must deploy capital across three separate protocols — a DEX, a money market, and a yield aggregator. Each protocol holds its own isolated pool. Capital allocated to Uniswap cannot simultaneously earn lending interest on Aave. Capital earning yield on Yearn cannot participate in DEX fee generation. The result is that the total liquidity available to any single user interaction is always a fraction of the total capital committed to that asset pair across the ecosystem.

This fragmentation creates shallow markets, high slippage on large trades, and artificially low yields for LPs because their capital serves only one master at a time. FLUX collapses all three functions into one pool, meaning the same dollar of capital simultaneously earns from swaps, lending, and hedging without being split or forced to choose.

2

Impermanent Loss

Impermanent loss (IL) is the structural penalty paid by AMM liquidity providers when the price of assets in a pool diverges from the price at which they deposited. When a trader swaps heavily in one direction, the pool's composition changes, and the LP ends up holding more of the asset that decreased in value and less of the asset that increased. The loss is "impermanent" only if prices return to their original ratio — which in practice may never happen.

For stablecoin pairs, IL is much smaller than for volatile asset pairs, but it still exists when exchange rates move — precisely the scenario FLUX's institutional users face. More importantly, standard AMMs provide no structural mechanism to offset IL. FLUX addresses this by providing LPs with additional revenue streams — lending interest and hedge execution fees — that generate income specifically when the pool is being heavily utilised, which is also when IL is most likely to occur. The offset is structural, not incidental.

3

Institutional FX Exposure

The global foreign exchange market processes approximately $9.6 trillion in daily volume. Yet the infrastructure serving this market is profoundly inefficient for anyone outside the largest financial institutions. Traditional FX hedging through banks carries fees of 2–5% of transaction value, requires 3–5 business days for settlement, operates only during banking hours, and is inaccessible to companies below certain minimum transaction thresholds.

For the mid-market company earning euros but paying dollar-denominated costs, the DAO holding multi-currency treasury reserves, and the fintech processing cross-border stablecoin payments, these constraints represent a real and ongoing cost. No viable onchain alternative has existed — until now. FLUX provides programmable, permissionless, always-on FX hedging at a fraction of the cost, settling in under a second, available to any organisation regardless of size or banking relationships.

4

Capital Inefficiency

In concentrated liquidity AMMs, LP capital that sits in bins far from the current price earns nothing. In traditional money markets, capital earns only when borrowed — which fluctuates with demand. In both cases, significant portions of deployed capital are idle at any given moment, earning no return for their owners.

FLUX's auto-lending mechanism ensures that idle LP capital — bins not currently being traded through — is automatically made available to borrowers. The transition is seamless and requires no action from the LP. Capital earns lending interest until it re-enters the active trading range, at which point it returns to earning swap fees. The LP never has capital sitting completely idle. This persistent yield underlies the IL offset and is a fundamental part of why FLUX LP positions are structurally more attractive than those in competing protocols.

5

Gated Hedging Access

Institutional-grade FX risk management tools — forwards, options, swaps — require established bank relationships, credit facilities, ISDA master agreements, and minimum deal sizes that effectively exclude all but the largest organisations. A company processing $2 million in monthly cross-border stablecoin payments cannot access the same hedging instruments available to Goldman Sachs.

FLUX makes these instruments accessible to any organisation with a wallet. Fixed-term borrowing with no liquidation risk, bin-based limit orders, automated stop-losses, and programmable vault hedging are all available to any treasury team, DAO, or fintech with stablecoins to deploy. The protocol does not know or care whether its user is a $500 billion asset manager or a $5 million startup. The infrastructure is permissionless, the pricing is transparent, and the execution is automatic.

The Common Thread

All five problems share the same root cause: capital that is allowed to do only one thing at a time. A dollar in a DEX cannot lend. A hedge in a bank cannot earn yield while it waits. An LP position cannot protect against IL from its own pool activity. FLUX's fused architecture removes these artificial boundaries by making the same capital pool simultaneously serve multiple functions — turning five separate problems into one unified solution.

1.4 — Introduction

Key Concepts

Before reading the protocol overview or any interaction guide, it helps to have a clear mental model of the core terms and primitives that appear throughout these docs. This page defines each concept plainly, with enough context to understand why it matters — not just what it is.

Note

These definitions are intentionally accessible rather than mathematically precise. Formal definitions with full derivations appear in Section 5 — Protocol Mathematics.

Pool Primitives

Fused Pool

The single liquidity pool per currency pair that powers all of FLUX's core functions. Unlike protocols that separate DEX and money market pools, FLUX's fused pool handles swapping, lending, and hedging within the same capital reserve. LPs deposit into the fused pool and earn from all three activities simultaneously.

Bin

A discrete price level within the pool. The pool is divided into many bins, each representing a specific exchange rate (e.g., the 1.08 bin means 1 EURC = 1.08 USDC). Liquidity deposited at a specific bin earns fees only from activity that occurs at that bin's price level. Bins above the active price hold the base asset. Bins below hold the quote asset.

Active Bin

The bin corresponding to the current market exchange rate. The active bin contains a mix of both assets and is where live trading happens. As swaps execute, the active bin may deplete and the protocol automatically activates the nearest adjacent bin to continue filling the trade — creating continuous price discovery.

X-Range

The set of bins positioned above the active bin. These bins hold the base asset (e.g., EURC in a USDC/EURC pool). They represent what LPs are willing to sell the base asset for if the price rises to those levels. X-range bins are available for borrowing by users seeking base asset exposure.

Y-Range

The set of bins positioned below the active bin. These bins hold the quote asset (e.g., USDC in a USDC/EURC pool). They represent what LPs are willing to accept for the base asset if the price falls to those levels. Y-range bins are available for borrowing by users seeking quote asset exposure.

Bin Step

The percentage price difference between adjacent bins. Bin prices follow a geometric progression — each bin is a fixed percentage higher or lower than the next. A smaller bin step means more granular price precision. A larger bin step means fewer bins across the same price range. The bin step is a pool parameter set at creation.

The FTL System

FTL

The Fused Trade Ledger. FLUX's core AMM architecture. The FTL is the design pattern that unifies the DEX and money market functions into one pool. It handles the bin-based segmentation of liquidity, the automatic transition of idle capital to lending, the debt management module, and fee accounting across all pool activity.

Auto-Lending

The mechanism by which LP capital that is not currently in an active trading range is automatically made available to borrowers. When the market price moves away from a bin where an LP has deposited, that bin's liquidity transitions from "active trading" status to "available for borrowing" status. The LP earns lending interest rather than swap fees during this period. No action is required from the LP.

Debt Reserve (Z)

A per-bin collateral reserve that holds assets posted by borrowers. When a borrower opens a position, their collateral is stored in the debt reserve of the specific bin they selected. The debt reserve has two components: Zx (storing base asset collateral) and Zy (storing quote asset collateral). These reserves ensure that collateral is available for immediate absorption if a debt expires.

Buffer Range

A protected zone of bins immediately surrounding the active bin, within which borrowing is prohibited. The buffer range exists to ensure that liquidity close to the current market price remains available for swaps and is not locked as debt collateral. This prevents scenarios where heavy borrowing near the active price degrades trading liquidity for the broader pool.

Borrowing & Debt

Debt NFT

An ERC-721 token minted to a borrower when they open a position. The Debt NFT is a verifiable onchain record of the debt, encoding the borrow bin price, borrow direction (above or below market), collateral value, and term expiry. Debt NFTs are transferable — positions can be sold on secondary markets before expiry. Repayment burns the NFT and releases the collateral.

Fixed-Term Borrowing

All borrowing positions in FLUX have a fixed 7-day term. At expiry, the position must be repaid or rolled over. Unlike perpetual borrowing in traditional money markets, there is no ongoing liquidation risk based on collateral ratio. The worst case is that the debt expires and the collateral is absorbed into the pool — a predictable, bounded outcome known in advance.

Debt Rollover

A mechanism allowing borrowers to extend their debt's expiry by another 7 days by paying a small rollover fee. Rollover resets the expiry countdown. Greyed debts (those within the buffer range) cannot be rolled over but can still be repaid. Inactive debts (where market price has crossed the borrow price) cannot be rolled over or repaid.

Auto-Liquidation

The protocol's debt recovery mechanism. When a debt expires without repayment, any user (the "blacklistoor") can trigger its liquidation. The protocol automatically absorbs the collateral into the corresponding bin's reserves, effectively returning it to the LP pool. The blacklistoor earns FLUX protocol rewards for performing this service. There are no liquidation bots or oracle-based price triggers — only time-based expiry.

Greyed Debt

A debt whose selected bin has been brought within the buffer range by market price movement. Greyed debts can still be repaid but cannot be rolled over. This state warns borrowers that they are approaching a zone where rollover protection is unavailable.

Inactive Debt

A debt where market price movement has caused a reversal — a position borrowed below the active price has crossed above it, or vice versa. Inactive debts cannot be repaid or rolled over but continue counting down to expiry. This state reflects that the borrower's assumed market direction was wrong and the position is effectively stranded until it expires.

Institutional Layer

Hedging Vault

A programmable smart contract that institutional users configure to manage their FX exposure automatically. A vault holds stablecoin deposits and monitors the holder's currency exposure in real time. When configured thresholds are breached, the vault executes hedging trades automatically through Arc's StableFX engine. Parameters include exposure limits, rate triggers, target currency ratios, and rebalancing frequency.

StableFX

Circle's institutional-grade FX engine built natively on Arc. StableFX uses an off-chain Request for Quote (RFQ) process for price discovery — familiar to institutional FX desks — with on-chain PvP settlement for atomic execution. Both legs of a currency conversion either settle simultaneously or neither settles, eliminating counterparty risk. FLUX vaults route institutional hedges through StableFX.

PvP Settlement

Payment-versus-Payment settlement. A settlement mechanism where both legs of a bilateral transaction settle simultaneously on-chain, guaranteed atomically by the smart contract. If either leg cannot settle, the entire transaction reverts. This eliminates the scenario in traditional FX where one party delivers funds before the other, creating settlement risk.

RFQ

Request for Quote. An off-chain price discovery mechanism used by StableFX where an institution requests price quotes from multiple market makers simultaneously. Market makers compete to offer the best rate. The institution accepts the best quote and the trade then settles on-chain. This is the same mechanism used by institutional FX desks globally, now with onchain settlement.

Documentation Conventions

Throughout these docs, the following conventions are used consistently.

Callout boxes

Shaded boxes like those appearing in this section highlight important notes, technical clarifications, access requirements, and warnings. Gold-bordered boxes are informational. Blue boxes are technical notes. Yellow boxes are warnings about access requirements or important constraints.

Worked examples

Numerical examples throughout the Core Interactions and Strategic Interactions sections use consistent pool parameters — a USDC/EURC pool with an active bin at 1.08, bin step of 0.2%, and standard fee parameters — unless otherwise stated. This allows examples to be compared directly across sections.

Diagrams

Architecture diagrams use dark backgrounds with gold highlights matching the FLUX visual identity. Pool state diagrams show bins as horizontal bars with the active bin highlighted in gold. Flow diagrams show transaction paths from left to right.

Inline code

Function names, contract addresses, parameter names, and code references appear in monospace formatting. Full code blocks appear in dedicated developer reference sections.

Mathematical notation

Section 5 uses standard mathematical notation. Variables are defined at first use and collected in a reference table at the start of each mathematics page. Where formulas are derived from Nitro Finance's FTL architecture, the derivation is noted explicitly.
